Introduction: From Caudillos to Institutions

This final section covers the consolidation of the post-revolutionary state and its eventual transformation. If the previous era was defined by violence and armed warlords (caudillos), this era is defined by the creation of institutions and the dominance of a single political party. Aboites Aguilar describes a journey from a rural, illiterate, and agrarian Mexico in 1929 to an urban, industrial, and increasingly democratic nation by 2000.

The "Mexican Miracle" (1940‚Äì1970)

Following C√°rdenas, the regime shifted to the right. World War II provided a golden opportunity for Mexico; the U.S. needed raw materials and labor, leading to the Bracero program and a boom in exports.

Successive presidents (√Åvila Camacho, Alem√°n, Ruiz Cortines, L√≥pez Mateos, and D√≠az Ordaz) focused on industrialization rather than agriculture. They adopted a policy of Import Substitution Industrialization (ISI), using high tariffs to protect domestic industries.

This period is known as the "Mexican Miracle" or "Stabilizing Development." The economy grew at an average of 6% annually for decades with low inflation. Mexico transformed from a rural country into an urban one, with Mexico City becoming a massive metropolis. The middle class expanded, gaining access to social security, universities, and housing. In 1946, the ruling party changed its name one last time to the Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI), symbolizing that the revolution was now a permanent government institution.

However, this stability came at a cost: Authoritarianism. The PRI maintained a monopoly on power. Opposition was tolerated only as a token; real power lay with the President. Unions and peasant organizations were co-opted and corrupt (charros).

The cracks in the system appeared in the late 1960s. The expanding middle class and university students began to demand democratic freedoms. This tension exploded in 1968, just before Mexico City hosted the Olympics. The government, intolerant of dissent, massacred student protesters at the Plaza de Tlatelolco on October 2. This tragedy destroyed the moral legitimacy of the PRI, separating the state from the intellectual class and the youth.

The Neoliberal Turn and Transition (1982‚Äì2000)

The crisis of 1982 ushered in a new group of leaders: the Technocrats. These were economists often educated in the U.S. (Ivy League), who believed the protectionist, state-heavy model was obsolete. Presidents Miguel de la Madrid, Carlos Salinas de Gortari, and Ernesto Zedillo dismantled the revolutionary nationalist state.

They implemented Neoliberal policies:

  • Privatization of hundreds of state-owned companies (including the banks and the telephone company).
  • Opening the country to foreign trade, culminating in the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA/TLCAN) in 1994.
  • Modification of Article 27 to end land redistribution and allow the sale of ejido land.

The widespread belief in fraud galvanized civil society.

1994 proved to be a traumatic year. On January 1, the day NAFTA went into effect, the Zapatista Army of National Liberation (EZLN) rose up in Chiapas, highlighting the poverty of indigenous people left behind by neoliberalism. Later that year, the PRI's presidential candidate, Luis Donaldo Colosio, was assassinated. Shortly after Ernesto Zedillo took office, the economy crashed again (the "December Mistake"), causing the peso to lose half its value.

The End of the System

The economic crises and corruption scandals made democratic change inevitable. President Zedillo implemented electoral reforms that gave true independence to the Federal Electoral Institute (IFE).

In the 2000 election, for the first time in 71 years, the PRI lost. Vicente Fox, the candidate of the conservative National Action Party (PAN), won the presidency. Aboites Aguilar concludes that while the arrival of democracy was a monumental achievement, the new millennium found Mexico facing old and persistent challenges: deep poverty, inequality, violence, and the difficult integration into a globalized world. The book ends with the image of a Mexico that has modernized and democratized, but has yet to solve the fundamental issues of social justice that sparked the Revolution a century earlier.
