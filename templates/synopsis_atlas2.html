2. Sociedad

While Section 1 described the container (the land), Section 2 describes the contents: the Mexican people. This section is critical for the Naturalization Exam because it covers Demographics, Indigenous Culture, Migration, and the Constitutional definition of Mexican identity.

Introduction: A Pluricultural Nation

The most important concept in this section comes directly from Article 2 of the Constitution: Mexico is defined as a "Naci√≥n Pluricultural" (Pluricultural Nation). Its identity is founded originally on its indigenous peoples, blended with European heritage (Mestizaje), and enriched by African and Asian migration.

  • The Institution: The data in this section comes principally from INEGI (Instituto Nacional de Estad√≠stica y Geograf√≠a), which conducts the national census every 10 years.

1. Population Dynamics (Demograf√≠a)

Mexico is the most populous Spanish-speaking country in the world and ranks among the top 10-11 most populous nations globally.

A. Total Population

  • According to the 2020 Census, Mexico has approximately 126 million inhabitants.
  • Gender Split: There are slightly more women (51.2%) than men (48.8%).
  • Life Expectancy (Esperanza de Vida): It has risen dramatically over the last century. Currently, the average is around 75 years (Women live roughly 6 years longer than men).

B. Age Structure

Mexico is currently experiencing a "Bono Demogr√°fico" (Demographic Dividend). This means the majority of the population is of working age (adults and young adults), though the population pyramid is slowly aging as birth rates decline.

  • Historical Context: In the 1970s, Mexican families were large (6-7 children). Today, the average is roughly 2 children per family.

2. Population Distribution

The Atlas highlights that the Mexican population is extremely unevenly distributed.

A. Density (Densidad de Poblaci√≥n)

  • High Density: The population is heavily concentrated in the center of the country (The Volcanic Belt). The State of Mexico and Mexico City have the highest population densities.
  • Low Density: The North (Deserts) and parts of the South (Jungles) are sparsely populated. Baja California Sur is the state with the lowest population density.

B. Urban vs. Rural

There has been a massive shift in where Mexicans live over the last 80 years.

  • 1950: The majority of Mexicans lived in rural towns (el campo).
  • Today: Nearly 80% of Mexicans live in Urban Areas (cities).
  • Definition: INEGI generally defines "Urban" as a town with more than 2,500 inhabitants, but the focus for the exam is on the massive "Zonas Metropolitanas."

C. The Three Giants

As noted in previous lists, the population is anchored by three massive metropolitan hubs:

  1. Valle de M√©xico (CDMX + Edo Mex): The political and financial heart.
  2. Guadalajara: The cultural center of the West.
  3. Monterrey: The industrial capital of the North.

4. Migration (Migraci√≥n)

The Atlas divides migration into two flows: Internal and External.

A. Internal Migration

People moving within Mexico.

  • The Trend: The dominant flow is from South to North and from Rural to Urban.
  • The Causes: People leave states with lower economic development (Chiapas, Guerrero, Oaxaca) to seek employment in industrial states (Nuevo Le√≥n, Baja California) or tourism hubs (Quintana Roo/Riviera Maya).

B. External Migration (Emigration)

People leaving Mexico.

  • The Destination: Over 95% of Mexican emigrants go to the United States.
  • Remittances (Remesas): Money sent back by Mexicans living abroad is one of the top sources of income for the Mexican economy.

C. Immigration (People coming to Mexico)

Historically, Mexico has welcomed refugees (Spanish exiles in the 30s, South Americans in the 70s). Today, there is a growing population of Americans (retirees) and distinct communities like the Mennonites in Chihuahua.

5. Heritage and Symbols

The "Society" section also touches on what binds these diverse people together.

A. National Symbols

  • The Flag: Three colors (Green=Hope, White=Unity, Red=Blood of heroes).
  • The Shield (Escudo Nacional): Based on the Aztec legend of the founding of Tenochtitlan (Eagle devouring a snake on a cactus).
  • The Anthem (Himno Nacional): Written by Bocanegra (Lyrics) and Nun√≥ (Music). It is a war anthem emphasizing defense of the homeland.

B. Traditions

  • D√≠a de Muertos (Day of the Dead): A UNESCO Intangible Heritage. It is a syncretism (mix) of Catholic and Pre-Hispanic beliefs.
  • Gastronomy: Mexican food is also UNESCO heritage. The "Holy Trinity" of the Mexican diet is Corn, Beans, and Chile (Ma√≠z, Frijol y Chile).

6. Social Indicators: Education and Health

The Atlas provides the current status of social rights guaranteed by the Constitution.

A. Education

  • Literacy (Alfabetismo): Mexico has drastically reduced illiteracy, though challenges remain in older populations and rural indigenous communities.

B. Health

  • Article 4: Guarantees the right to health protection.
