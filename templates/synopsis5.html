Economic Modernization

The "Progress" aspect of the regime was its most successful claim to legitimacy. The Porfiriato transformed Mexico from a bankrupt, isolated nation into a participant in the global economy.

1. Foreign Investment and Infrastructure
The regime created a legal framework that was extremely favorable to foreign investors, primarily from the United States, Great Britain, and France. The most transformative achievement was the expansion of the railroad network. In 1876, Mexico had less than 500 kilometers of track; by 1910, it had nearly 20,000 kilometers. This integrated a national market for the first time, allowing goods to move from the north to the center and out to the ports.

2. Mining and Industry
New technologies revived the mining sector. While silver remained important, the production of industrial metals (copper, lead, zinc) exploded to feed the factories of the U.S. and Europe. The petroleum industry also began on the Gulf Coast during this period. Manufacturing grew in cities like Mexico City, Monterrey, and Puebla, focusing on textiles, beer, and glass.

Social Contrasts and Inequality

While the macroeconomic numbers were impressive, the benefits were distributed unevenly. The Porfiriato was a time of profound social inequality.

  • The Elites and Middle Class: A wealthy oligarchy emerged, consisting of landowners, bankers, and industrialists who lived in luxury, mimicking French fashion and culture. A small but growing middle class (lawyers, doctors, teachers, bureaucrats) also developed in the cities. While they benefited from the stability, they eventually grew resentful of their exclusion from political decision-making.
  • The Agrarian Problem: The countryside saw the dramatic expansion of the Hacienda system. Laws regarding "vacant lands" (terrenos bald√≠os) allowed surveying companies to seize land held by indigenous communities that lacked proper documentation. This led to a massive concentration of land ownership; by 1910, a few thousand families owned the vast majority of arable land, while millions of peasants lived as landless laborers.
  • Labor Conditions: In the haciendas, workers were often tied to the land through debt peonage at the tienda de raya (company store). In the new factories and mines, workers faced long hours, low wages, and dangerous conditions. When workers tried to organize, they were met with violence.
