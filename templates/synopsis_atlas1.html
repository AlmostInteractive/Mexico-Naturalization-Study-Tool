Introduction: The Shape and Position of Mexico

Mexico is geographically positioned as a bridge between the temperate lands of North America and the tropical lands of Central America. This unique position creates a "mega-diverse" environment.

  • Location: Mexico is located in the Northern and Western Hemispheres.
  • The Borders:

    • Southeast: Guatemala (956 km) and Belize (193 km).
    • West: The Pacific Ocean (Mexico‚Äôs longest coastline).
    • East: The Gulf of Mexico and the Caribbean Sea (part of the Atlantic Ocean).
  • Total Area: Roughly 1.9 million square kilometers.

1. Relief (Orography and Mountains)

The Atlas emphasizes that Mexico is an extremely mountainous country. The land is defined by major mountain ranges (Sierras) that encircle the central plateau.

A. The Sierra Madres

  • Sierra Madre Occidental: Runs along the west (Pacific side) from the US border down to Jalisco. It is a continuation of the Rocky Mountains. It is famous for deep canyons, most notably the Barrancas del Cobre (Copper Canyon).
  • Sierra Madre Oriental: Runs along the east (Gulf side). These are folded mountains made largely of limestone.
  • Sierra Madre del Sur: A complex, rugged range covering Michoac√°n, Guerrero, and Oaxaca. It is separated from the other ranges, making infrastructure difficult in the south.

B. The Central Plateau (Altiplano Mexicano)

Between the two northern Sierras lies a vast, high-altitude plateau.

  • The North: Arid, lower altitude (Deserts).
  • The South (Mesa Central): Higher altitude, temperate climate. This is the heart of the country where Mexico City is located.

C. The Trans-Mexican Volcanic Belt (Eje Neovolc√°nico)

This is a crucial geological feature for the exam. It is a chain of volcanoes that crosses the country from west to east (roughly from Nayarit to Veracruz). It marks the geological dividing line between North and Central America. It houses Mexico‚Äôs highest peaks:

  1. Pico de Orizaba (Citlalt√©petl): The highest point in Mexico (5,636 meters). Located between Puebla and Veracruz.
  2. Popocat√©petl: An active volcano visible from Mexico City.

  4. Paricut√≠n: famous for being the youngest volcano in the world, which emerged suddenly in a cornfield in Michoac√°n in 1943.

D. The Coastal Plains

  • Pacific Coastal Plain: Narrow and broken by mountains.

  • Yucat√°n Platform: A flat limestone shelf with almost no surface rivers; water runs underground (cenotes).

3. Climates

The Atlas explains that Mexico‚Äôs climate is determined by two main factors: Latitude and Altitude.

A. Latitude (The Tropic of Cancer)

The Tropic of Cancer cuts through the middle of Mexico (near Mazatl√°n and Tamaulipas).

  • North of the line: Generally dry/arid with extreme seasonal temperature changes (hot summers, cold winters).
  • South of the line: Generally tropical with constant warm temperatures and rainy seasons.

B. Altitude (The Vertical Factor)

Even in the tropical south, the high mountains create temperate or cold zones.

  • Tierra Caliente (0-1,000m): Coastal plains and the Yucat√°n. Hot year-round.
  • Tierra Templada (1,000-2,000m): The climate of "Eternal Spring." Cities like Cuernavaca and Guadalajara.
  • Tierra Fr√≠a (2,000m+): The high valleys. Mexico City, Toluca, Puebla. Cool nights, mild days.

C. Rainfall Patterns

  • Wettest Region: The Southeast (Chiapas, Tabasco, Veracruz) receives rain almost year-round.
  • Driest Region: The Northwest (Baja California, Sonora) is desert.
  • Seasonal Rain: Most of the central country (Mexico City) has a distinct rainy season from June to October.
