Introduction: An Open, Mixed Economy

The Atlas portrays Mexico as a developing industrial nation with a mixed economy (a combination of private enterprise and state-owned strategic sectors like energy).

  • Global Position: Mexico is a member of the G20 (the top 20 economies in the world) and the OECD.
  • Orientation: It is an export-oriented economy. Mexico has more Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) than almost any other country, functioning as a manufacturing hub for the world.
  • The Key Partner: The United States. Roughly 80% of Mexico‚Äôs exports go to the USA. The governing document for this relationship is the T-MEC (Tratado entre M√©xico, Estados Unidos y Canad√°), formerly known as TLCAN/NAFTA.

1. The Primary Sector (Agriculture and Livestock)

Although it contributes a smaller percentage to the GDP than industry, this sector is historically and culturally foundational.

A. Agriculture (Agricultura)

The Atlas divides agriculture into two types:

  • Subsistence (Temporal): Relying on rain (mostly in the Center and South). Corn and beans for local consumption.
  • Commercial (Riego): Relying on irrigation (mostly in the North and Baj√≠o). High-value crops for export.

B. "Green Gold" and Key Crops

Mexico is the world leader in several agricultural products:

  • Avocado (Aguacate): Michoac√°n is the world capital of avocado production.
  • Tomatoes & Berries: Massive exports from Sinaloa and Jalisco to the US winter market.
  • Corn (Ma√≠z): The cultural staple. While Mexico produces White Corn (for tortillas), it still imports Yellow Corn (for livestock feed).

C. Livestock (Ganader√≠a)

  • Cattle (Bovino): The North (Sonora, Chihuahua) is famous for high-quality beef export. The tropical Southeast (Veracruz, Tabasco) also has large herds but focuses more on volume than premium cuts.
  • Poultry and Pork: Concentrated in the center (Jalisco, Puebla) to feed the massive urban populations.

2. The Extractive Sector (Mining and Oil)

Mexico is historically a mining country (dating back to the Colonial era).

A. Mining (Miner√≠a)

  • Silver: Mexico has been the #1 producer of silver in the world for centuries. The "Silver Belt" runs through Zacatecas (the top producer), Durango, and Chihuahua.
  • Copper: Cananea (Sonora) is one of the largest open-pit copper mines in the world.
  • Lithium: Recently declared a strategic mineral owned by the nation; deposits are located in Sonora.

B. Oil (Petr√≥leo)

  • PEMEX: Petr√≥leos Mexicanos is the state-owned company.
  • Location: The vast majority of extraction happens in the Gulf of Mexico (specifically the Sonda de Campeche) and the coastal plains of Tabasco and Veracruz.
  • Impact: While oil exports have declined as a percentage of GDP compared to the 1980s, oil revenue still funds a significant portion of the federal budget.

3. The Secondary Sector (Industry and Manufacturing)

This is the engine of the modern Mexican economy. The Atlas highlights the concept of the "Maquiladora": factories (often near the border) that import raw materials tariff-free, assemble them, and export the finished product.

A. The Automotive Industry

Mexico is a global powerhouse in car manufacturing (Top 10 in the world).

  • Electronics: Tijuana and Guadalajara (often called the "Mexican Silicon Valley") are major producers of TVs and appliances.

C. Food and Beverage

  • Beer: Mexico is the #1 exporter of beer in the world (Corona, Modelo).
  • Tequila/Mezcal: Protected by "Denomination of Origin." Tequila can only be produced in Jalisco and specific neighbors; Mezcal is centered in Oaxaca.

4. The Tertiary Sector (Services, Commerce, and Tourism)

This sector employs the largest number of Mexicans (over 60% of the workforce).

A. Tourism (Turismo)

Often called the "Industry without Chimneys" (La industria sin chimeneas). It is the third or fourth largest source of foreign currency.

  • Sun and Beach: Canc√∫n, Riviera Maya, Los Cabos, Puerto Vallarta.

B. Commerce and The Informal Economy

The Atlas acknowledges a structural challenge: Informality. A large percentage of the economic activity (street vendors, unregistered businesses) is not taxed or regulated, meaning millions of workers lack social security (IMSS).

5. Economic Regions and Inequality

The most critical geographic lesson in this section is the North-South Economic Divide.

A. The North (The Industrial Powerhouse)

  • States like Nuevo Le√≥n, Chihuahua, and Baja California.
  • Characteristics: High GDP per capita, heavy industrialization, high export volume, lower poverty rates. Highly integrated with the US economy.

B. The Center (The Consumer Hub)

  • CDMX, Edo Mex, Quer√©taro, Jalisco.
  • Characteristics: Service-oriented, banking, corporate headquarters, and high-tech manufacturing.

C. The South (The Agricultural/Resource Base)

  • Chiapas, Oaxaca, Guerrero.
  • Characteristics: Rich in natural resources (water, electricity, biodiversity) but historically poor in infrastructure and industrialization.
  • Result: These states have the highest rates of poverty and are the primary source of internal migration.

6. External Income: Remittances (Remesas)

The Atlas and economic texts highlight a unique economic pillar: Remittances.

  • Definition: Money sent by Mexican migrants living in the United States back to their families in Mexico.
  • Importance: Along with Oil, Tourism, and Auto Exports, Remittances are one of the top sources of foreign income for the country, often serving as a lifeline for rural communities in the Baj√≠o and South.
