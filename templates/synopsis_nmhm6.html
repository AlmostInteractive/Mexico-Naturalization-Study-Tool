Introduction: A Violent Transformation

The author presents the Mexican Revolution not as a single, unified event, but as a complex sequence of conflicts involving distinct regions, social classes, and political projects. What began as a political rebellion by the middle and upper classes to establish democracy evolved into a profound social upheaval driven by peasants and workers, finally concluding with the establishment of a new, powerful state led by a victorious middle-class military faction from the north.

With the common enemy gone, the revolutionary coalition fractured. The question arose: What kind of new nation would be built?

  • The Conventionists: Villa and Zapata met at the Convention of Aguascalientes. They represented the popular, agrarian, and regionalist wing of the Revolution. The Constitution of 1917

Victorious, Carranza convened a congress in Quer√©taro to draft a new constitution. While Carranza presented a moderate draft, the radical "Jacobin" wing of his followers pushed for a document that went much further. The resulting Constitution of 1917 was the most advanced in the world at the time. It enshrined the demands of the Revolution into law:

  • Article 3: Established secular, state-controlled education, limiting the power of the Church.
  • Article 27: Declared that the nation was the original owner of all lands and waters. It authorized the state to seize land for redistribution (land reform) and asserted national ownership of subsoil resources (oil and minerals), challenging foreign companies.
  • Article 123: Established radical labor rights, including the eight-hour workday, the right to strike, and a minimum wage.

Conclusion: The End of the Armed Struggle

The death of Carranza and the ascent of the Sonoran group marked the end of the violent phase of the Revolution. The Sonorans were pragmatists who understood the need to rebuild the state and economy. They incorporated the surviving Zapatistas and other factions into the government, pacified the country, and began the long process of institutionalizing the Revolution. The era of warlords was ending; the era of institutions was beginning.
